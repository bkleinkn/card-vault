About Card Vault

Card Vault helps you figure out what your sports cards are and roughly what they may be worth. Take a photo, the app identifies the card using AI, and you can save it to a personal collection.

Going through an inherited collection?  Read the inheritance guide first — it covers what to do (and not do) before you sell, clean, or grade anything.

How to scan well

  • Lay the card on a flat, non-shiny surface.
  • Use good light — natural daylight is best.
  • Fill the frame with the card.
  • Make sure the player's name and the year are easy to read.

How the value estimate is calculated

Card Vault uses Claude, an AI model from Anthropic, to identify the card and produce a rough value range. The estimate comes from the AI's training knowledge of historical card values — it does not query a live pricing database in real time.

Plain language: the dollar range is a ballpark from the AI's general awareness, not a current market quote. The AI knows that, say, a 1956 Topps Mantle has historically traded in a certain range, but it can't see what the most recent one sold for yesterday.

Real pricing databases that exist (and that future versions of Card Vault may integrate):

  • eBay – Sold Listings — the gold standard for actual recent transactions
  • PriceCharting — comprehensive sports card price guide
  • Card Ladder — tracks card values over time like stocks
  • 130point.com — aggregates eBay sold data
  • TCDB (Trading Card Database) — community-maintained card catalog

How to verify before buying or selling

Always check recent eBay Sold Items for your specific card. Search the year, set, player, and card number (for example, "1956 Topps Mickey Mantle 135") and filter to Sold Items. The most recent sales are the most accurate.

When to call a professional

For any single card that might be worth $500 or more, get a second opinion before selling. Reputable card-grading companies will give you an official condition grade and a more reliable value:

  • PSA (Professional Sports Authenticator) — the most widely recognized sports card grading
  • SGC (Sportscard Guaranty) — popular for vintage cards
  • BGS (Beckett Grading Services) — precise sub-grades, often preferred for modern cards

For especially high-value cards (estimate $5,000+), consider consigning to a major auction house like Goldin, Heritage Auctions, or Memory Lane.

Your privacy

Your card photos and collection are stored privately under your account on Google's Firebase platform. Only you can see them. Card images are sent to Anthropic's Claude API for identification, then immediately forgotten by the model — Anthropic does not retain them or train on them (see their usage policy).

Inherited a collection? Start here.

Vintage cards are easy to undervalue and easy to ruin. Read this before you sell, clean, or grade anything.

1. Don't sell the whole collection in bulk

Bulk buyers and shops pay a fraction of actual value to leave room for their own profit and grading risk. Watch out especially for:

  • People offering cash without examining cards carefully
  • Self-described “estate buyers”
  • Flea market dealers
  • Facebook Marketplace flippers

A collection like this deserves at least a basic sort and evaluation first.

2. Separate by decade and manufacturer first

Before worrying about exact values, organize into decades:

  • 1930s
  • 1940s
  • 1950s
  • 1960s & up

Then within each decade, group by manufacturer:

  • Topps
  • Bowman
  • Goudey
  • Play Ball
  • Leaf

The card backs usually identify the company and year.

3. Pull out Hall of Famers immediately

This is where most of the value sits. Examples from the vintage eras:

  • Babe Ruth
  • Mickey Mantle
  • Jackie Robinson
  • Ted Williams
  • Willie Mays
  • Hank Aaron
  • Joe DiMaggio
  • Stan Musial

Even well-worn examples of these names can be extremely valuable.

4. Condition matters enormously

The same card can go from $50 in poor shape to $5,000+ graded in high condition. Things that matter:

  • Centering (is the image centered on the card?)
  • Sharp corners (no rounding or wear)
  • Creases or bends
  • Surface damage or scratches
  • Writing or marks
  • Stains
  • Edge wear (chipping along the borders)

Do NOT clean them. Ever. Seriously. People destroy enormous value trying to “improve” vintage cards. The patina and surface are part of what makes them authentic.

5. The smart sequence

  1. Use Card Vault to identify potentially valuable cards
  2. Send only the best for professional grading

The major grading companies are PSA, SGC, and BGS (Beckett). For vintage baseball, PSA and SGC are the biggest names and command the strongest resale prices.

6. Selling options — ranked by money vs. effort

Highest possible money (most time and effort):

Sell individually after grading, via eBay, major auction houses, or directly to serious collectors. The major auction houses for high-end vintage:

  • Heritage Auctions
  • REA (Robert Edward Auctions)
  • Goldin Auctions

Takes the longest but maximizes proceeds.

Best balance of effort and money:

Use a consignment service. They identify key cards, handle grading, photograph, list, and sell — for a percentage of proceeds. Good when you don't want to handle the logistics yourself.

Fastest, easiest, lowest payout:

Sell the whole collection to a dealer. Quick cash, but usually a small fraction of actual value.

7. Before doing anything else

Take photos of:

  • Entire boxes and binders (overview shots)
  • Fronts and backs of any star cards

Then you can:

  • Compare against sold listings on eBay Sold Items
  • Post in collector groups for second opinions
  • Request free evaluations from auction houses (most will look at high-end material for free)

8. Originality and authenticity

Vintage cards have been heavily reprinted over the years. A legitimate 1952 Topps Mickey Mantle is life-changing money. A reprint is not.

This is another reason professional grading matters — grading companies authenticate before they grade. A graded card is a verified-real card.

When in doubt, slow down. Take a week. Vintage cards have been valuable for 70+ years — they'll still be valuable next month.
